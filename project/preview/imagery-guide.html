Imagery — the MyGig look

Real workers. Real worksites. Documentary, not stock. Imagery is the most important non-typographic carrier of the brand — it tells the trust story the copy keeps repeating. This page captures the system already in use on mygig.com.au and codifies how to extend it.

01The library — by industry

MyGig serves a wide range of industries, and the photo library reflects that. Every shoot covers a worker in their environment — never against a blank backdrop. Solo subjects, mid-action or warmly direct.

02Signature pattern · the hero portrait row

The hero photo row on mygig.com.au — six (or four–six) vertical portrait cards lined up under the headline. This is the brand's most recognisable imagery moment. Use it on home pages, section openers, "who we serve" blocks. Always with rounded corners (--r-lg / 18px) and a strict 9:19 (or ~9:16) aspect.

04Signature pattern · the 2-up split

The bread-and-butter layout for product/service blocks. Photo on one side (square-ish ratio, ~5:4), copy + CTA on the other. Photo carries the emotional weight; copy carries the proof.

08Do & don't
✓
Do show real workers in their environment — vineyard, kitchen, supermarket floor, construction site, forklift cab.
✕
Don't use blank-backdrop studio portraits or stock-photo handshakes.
✓
Do keep faces visible and the moment honest — direct portraits ok, mid-task ok. A genuine smile beats a posed one.
✕
Don't hide faces with motion blur, heavy crops, or stylised cropping that removes the human.
✓
Do chase warm, natural light — golden hour outdoors, daylight through windows, warm tungsten in hospo settings.
✕
Don't use cold blue tech lighting, neon glow, or sci-fi "future of work" aesthetics.
✓
Do include genuine industry uniforms — aprons, hi-vis, hard hats, chef whites. The wardrobe tells the story.
✕
Don't generate AI worker photography. We don't fake the workforce; that's the brand promise.
✓
Do represent the breadth of Australia's casual workforce — age, gender, ethnicity, ability — all real, all on the floor.
✕
Don't mono-cast a row of similar-looking faces. The hero row should look like a crew, not a clone set.
✓
Do shoot vertical (9:16 / 4:5) by default — most uses are portrait or stacked card.
✕
Don't crop a landscape frame into a square if it loses the worker's context.
09When you don't have a photo yet

Don't reach for stock or AI. Use a solid ink-900 block at the photo's intended dimensions, with a brand-300 corner label naming exactly what the shot will be (subject, role, location). This is the only sanctioned placeholder.
